The Problem

Keyword filters can't see what matters

What recruiters face

Hundreds of profiles, and the right person still gets missed — not because the talent isn't there, but because keyword matching is blind to context, trajectory, and intent.

What "fit" really means

The job needs someone who built retrieval & ranking systems — not someone who merely lists the words. Those are very different people.

The dataset is adversarial — on purpose

100K candidates seeded with keyword stuffers, plain-language strong fits, behavioural twins, and ~80 honeypots with subtly impossible profiles. A naïve embedding/keyword model walks straight into every trap.

Reading the Constraints

The limits are the design brief

ConstraintWhat it rules out
≤ 5 min · CPU · no network No hosted LLM call per candidate. 100K API calls won't fit, and the reproduction sandbox has no internet and no GPU.
100,000 candidates Per-candidate deep inference is too slow — needs a fast, streaming scoring pass.
Honeypot rate > 10% = DQ The model must actually read profiles, not embed keywords.
Stage 4–5: defend your work Every score must be explainable by a human, not a black box.

➡ A per-candidate GPT-4 pipeline is disqualified before it scores a single point. The winning shape is a fast, transparent, deterministic ranker.

Our Approach

A transparent hybrid ranker

Read the role, not the words

The JD is encoded as competency lexicons, weights and disqualifiers — what the role means, not just what it says.

Score the whole picture

Seven fit components × a behavioural-availability modifier × penalty factors for the JD's explicit disqualifiers.

Explain every pick

Each candidate gets specific, non-templated reasoning grounded only in facts from their profile.

score = Σ wᵢ·componentᵢ × behavioural_modifier × penalty_factors × honeypot_kill

Architecture

One streaming pass, bounded memory

candidates.jsonl→ stream record→ feature extraction→ fit scoring
behavioural modifier→ penalty / honeypot→ top-N heap→ reasoning→ submission.csv

Why streaming

We never hold all 100K parsed records in RAM — only a small heap of the strongest candidates. Peak memory stays far under 16 GB.

Why deterministic

Same input → same output, every run. Reproduces unchanged in the Stage-3 sandbox; defensible line-by-line at the interview.

The Fit Score

Seven components, every one explainable

ComponentWeightWhat it captures

Skills0.22Trust-weighted core-competency coverage — endorsements + months-of-use neutralise padded lists.

Beating the Traps

Two ideas that defeat the dataset

① Title × skill-trust

A keyword stuffer has a perfect skill list but a non-engineering title and skills with 0 endorsements / 0 months of use.

Title crushes the first; a per-skill trust multiplier discounts the second.

"Marketing Manager + 9 AI skills" → sinks. plain-language builder → rises.

② Consistency honeypot check

Honeypots are subtly impossible: 8 yrs tenure in a 3-year-old role; "expert" in 10 skills with 0 months used.

We detect the violations and floor the score.

Result: 0 honeypots in our top 100 (DQ threshold > 10%).

The provided sample_submission.csv ranks keyword stuffers at the top — it is a deliberately wrong format reference. We rank the opposite way.

Signal Integration

Is the candidate actually hire-able?

A perfect-on-paper profile inactive for 6 months with a 5% response rate is, for hiring, not available. The 23 Redrob behavioural signals become a multiplicative availability modifier (down to ~0.3×).

Availability

recruiter response rate · activity recency · open-to-work · notice period

Demand

saved by recruiters · search appearances · interview-completion rate

Trust

verified email/phone · profile completeness · LinkedIn connected

Penalty factors — the JD's explicit "do-not-want"

CV/speech-only without NLP/IR · research-only with no production · consulting-only career · title-chasing (<20-month tenures) · recent LangChain-only dabbler.
